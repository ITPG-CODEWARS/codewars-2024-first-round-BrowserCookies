The use of two booster sections instead of two head sections improves the working conditions of the locomotive crew due to the ability to pass through all sections during movement and reduces the overall cost of the locomotives due to the absence of unnecessary cabins.
The peculiarity of the electric locomotive in comparison with the serial 2ES5K and 3ES5K is the increased weight of the sections to 98 tons, the use of an improved microprocessor control system and rectifier-inverter converters, the same as on the experimental electric locomotive 3ES5K-434. In addition, while in one of the booster sections the space instead of a cabin is still occupied by a spacious toilet, in the other section it is allocated for a compartment with two sleeping berths for rest of the locomotive crew or accompanying persons and has a side window . In the 4ES5K electric locomotives from number 004 onwards, a busbar installation is used instead of power cable installation on all sections, in the second booster section with a compartment there is also no motor-compressor unit and main tanks on the roof

Entering service in 1965, the Class 40 also operated on private railroads after the repeal of CFRs monopoly on rail transport. The vast majority of all the engines are still in service, as of 2020, having become an icon for the Romanian Railways.

By the 1930s On Soviet railways, the requirements for traction and train speed increased significantly. The existing steam locomotives of type 0-5-0 (series E) and type 1-5-0 (series Ye), the adhesion weight of which did not exceed 85 tons, were no longer able to fully cope with the increased volume of transportation. There was an urgent need to replace them with much more powerful locomotives.
